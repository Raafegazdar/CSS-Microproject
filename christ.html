INTRODUCTION

Christ the Redeemer is a monumental statue of Jesus Christ in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Completed in 1931, it stands 98 feet tall on the Corcovado Mountain, overlooking the city. The statue is made of reinforced concrete and soapstone and is a symbol of Christianity and Brazilian culture. It is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World and a major tourist attraction, representing peace and welcoming all who visit Rio.

The purpose of Christ the Redeemer was to symbolize Christianity and to serve as a religious and cultural monument for Brazil. The idea for the statue emerged in the early 20th century as a response to the perceived threat of secularism and to mark the centenary of Brazil’s independence from Portugal. It was intended to be a visible and lasting symbol of faith, peace, and the welcoming spirit of the Brazilian people. Christ the Redeemer was named a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2012, not just for its religious significance but also for its cultural and historical value. The statue is an iconic representation of Rio de Janeiro and Brazil, reflecting the blending of natural beauty and human creativity. Its location on Corcovado Mountain, which offers stunning views of the city, further contributes to its global significance as a symbol of harmony between nature and human achievement.

Introduction

Christ the Redeemer is an iconic statue of Jesus Christ located in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Standing at 98 feet (30 meters) tall atop the Corcovado Mountain, it is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. Constructed between 1922 and 1931, the statue is made of reinforced concrete and soapstone. It represents a symbol of Christianity and is a cultural landmark that draws millions of visitors each year, offering panoramic views of the city. Christ the Redeemer is not only a religious icon but also a symbol of peace, love, and hospitality in Brazil.

Brief History

Christ the Redeemer was conceived in the 1920s to mark Brazil's centenary of independence and to symbolize Christianity. Designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by French artist Paul Landowski, the statue was constructed between 1922 and 1931 on Rio de Janeiro's Corcovado Mountain. Standing 98 feet tall, it is made of reinforced concrete and soapstone. Over time, it has become a symbol of peace and Brazilian identity. In 2007, it was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, and in 2012, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its cultural and historical significance.

Important Details

Christ the Redeemer, designed by Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by Paul Landowski, features outstretched arms symbolizing peace and Christianity. Made of reinforced concrete and covered with soapstone for durability, the statue stands 98 feet tall on Corcovado Mountain. It has become a global cultural icon, often featured in media as a symbol of Rio de Janeiro and Brazil. The statue has undergone multiple restorations, including the installation of lightning rods and modern LED lighting, which enhances its visibility and allows for special lighting effects on significant occasions.

The Timeline

Christ the Redeemer was built between 1922 and 1931. Construction began in 1922, coinciding with the centenary of Brazil's independence, and the statue was officially completed and inaugurated on October 12, 1931.

Building Cost

The construction cost of Christ the Redeemer was approximately 250,000 Brazilian réis at the time, which is roughly equivalent to $250,000 USD. This would be much higher in today's currency due to inflation. The project was funded primarily through donations from the Brazilian Catholic community.

Did You Know?

Lightning Strikes

Due to its height and location, Christ the Redeemer is frequently struck by lightning. In 2014, one strike damaged the tip of its right thumb, leading to repairs.

Invisible Repairs

The soapstone used for repairs is carefully selected to match the original color. However, the original quarry in Brazil has run out of this particular stone, making it increasingly difficult to find an exact match.

Symbolic Direction

The statue faces the east, towards the rising sun, symbolizing the resurrection of Christ and offering a protective gaze over the city of Rio de Janeiro.
